Manifold Learning

Manifold learning is a fascinating area of machine learning and statistics that focuses on non-linear dimensionality reduction. The core assumption is that the high-dimensional data we observe, let's say $X \in \mathbb{R}^D$, actually lies on a lower-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ of intrinsic dimension $d < D$.

The Manifold Hypothesis

The Manifold Hypothesis states that real-world high-dimensional data (like images, speech signals, etc.) lie on low-dimensional manifolds embedded within the high-dimensional space.

Mathematically, we are looking for a mapping $f: \mathcal{M} \to \mathbb{R}^d$ that preserves certain properties of the data, such as local geometry.

Mathematical Formulation

Given a set of points $\{x_1, \dots, x_N\} \subset \mathbb{R}^D$, we aim to find corresponding coordinates $\{y_1, \dots, y_N\} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$.

Isomap

Isomap (Isometric Mapping) tries to preserve the geodesic distances between all pairs of points. The geodesic distance $d_\mathcal{M}(x_i, x_j)$ is approximated by the shortest path distance in a nearest-neighbor graph.

The algorithm proceeds in three steps:

  1. Construct a neighborhood graph $G$.
  2. Compute shortest paths between all pairs to approximate geodesic distances.
  3. Use Multidimensional Scaling (MDS) to embed the data into $\mathbb{R}^d$.

Locally Linear Embedding (LLE)

LLE assumes that each data point and its neighbors lie on or close to a locally linear patch of the manifold. We can reconstruct each point $x_i$ from its neighbors $x_j$:

$$ x_i \approx \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} w_{ij} x_j $$

subject to $\sum_j w_{ij} = 1$. The weights $W$ characterize the local geometry, which is then preserved in the low-dimensional embedding.
